# Product Decision (Part 2)

The data showed a clear gap in satisfaction between AI responses that cited sources and those that didn't. Cited responses received positive feedback 81% of the time. Uncited responses received positive feedback 20% of the time. That's not a marginal difference. It's the difference between a tool users trust and one they don't. The qualitative feedback explained why: *"Twice now it's given me an answer that sounds completely authoritative and is just... not in the document. That's terrifying when you're advising a client on a £40M acquisition."* Another user: *"When it doesn't cite anything specific, I have to go find it myself anyway, so what's the point?"*

I considered a report export feature and better context handling, both of which had signal in the data. But neither addresses the root problem. If users don't trust the answers, they won't use the output in their work regardless of how easy it is to export. Each AI response shows numbered superscript badges inline. Hovering over a badge reveals the source document, page number, and the exact quoted passage. Clicking it opens the document viewer, navigates to that page, and highlights the text in yellow. A full source list also appears below each response so users can scan all references at a glance without clicking through each one. That's the bar legal professionals need before they can rely on a tool professionally.

With more time, I would focus on three things. First, capture which specific citations users flag as wrong, not just thumbs down on the whole response. That's the signal needed to improve accuracy over time. Second, handle large document sets more gracefully, since long conversations with multiple documents will eventually hit token limits. Third, build report generation so users can go from a conversation to a shareable due diligence document without copying and pasting, which is how most of them are currently getting value out of the tool.
